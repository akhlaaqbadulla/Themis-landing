Financial Services Law in Mauritius

The regulatory architecture behind Mauritius's international financial centre: licensing, banking, securities, and the AML/CFT framework that every transactional and compliance practitioner navigates daily.

The statutory backbone

  • Financial Services Act 2007: the Financial Services Commission's founding statute: licensing of non-bank financial services and the global business regime.
  • Banking Act 2004: banking licences and supervision under the Bank of Mauritius, alongside the Bank of Mauritius Act 2004.
  • Securities Act 2005: public offers, listings, market intermediaries, and market abuse.
  • Financial Intelligence and Anti-Money Laundering Act 2002 (FIAMLA): the AML/CFT core, with its regulations and the FIU's guidance.
  • Insurance Act 2005 and the Private Pension Schemes Act 2012: sectoral regimes under FSC supervision.
  • Data Protection Act 2017: increasingly central for licensees handling client data.

What the case law and practice keep deciding

  • Regulatory enforcement. Challenges to FSC decisions (licence revocations, directions, administrative penalties) and the review mechanisms available to licensees.
  • Global business substance. What tax residence and substance require of GBC structures, a question with domestic and treaty dimensions frequently tested in litigation.
  • AML/CFT compliance. The reach of customer due diligence and reporting duties, and the consequences of failures, an area reshaped after Mauritius's successful exit from the FATF grey list.
  • Banking disputes. Account freezes, banker's duties, and guarantees.
  • Cross-border insolvency and asset recovery involving Mauritian vehicles, often ending at the Privy Council.
