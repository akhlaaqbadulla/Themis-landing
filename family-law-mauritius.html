Family Law in Mauritius

Where the French civil-law heritage shows most clearly: marriage, divorce, filiation, and children — governed substantially by the Code Civil Mauricien and shaped by decades of Supreme Court jurisprudence.

The statutory backbone

  • Code Civil Mauricien — the heart of family law: marriage and its effects, matrimonial regimes, divorce grounds and consequences, filiation, parental authority, and succession. Much of it is applied in French, and its interpretation draws on French doctrine and jurisprudence.
  • Divorce and Judicial Separation Act — the procedural framework for dissolving marriages before the Supreme Court.
  • Children's Act 2020 — the modern framework for the protection and welfare of children, replacing earlier child-protection legislation.
  • Protection from Domestic Violence Act — protection orders and related remedies.

What the case law keeps deciding

  • Divorce grounds and fault. The contours of faute-based divorce and the evidential standards it demands, alongside the no-fault routes.
  • Custody (garde) and the child's interest. How courts weigh the interest of the child across residence, contact, and relocation disputes.
  • Maintenance (pension alimentaire). Quantification for spouses and children, variation when circumstances change, and enforcement.
  • Matrimonial property. The consequences of the chosen regime — communauté or separation of property — on dissolution and succession.
  • Filiation and adoption. Establishing and contesting parentage under the Code Civil's rules.

Family research is genuinely bilingual: statute and doctrine in French, procedure and many judgments in English — a combination that defeats generic AI tools entirely.
